Methods: MCO CONUS Drought Monitoring

Montana Climate Office — Operational Drought Metrics from gridMET

Version: 1.1

1. Overview

The Montana Climate Office (MCO) CONUS Drought Monitoring system computes a suite of drought and climate metrics on a daily basis across the contiguous United States. The system ingests daily gridded meteorological fields from gridMET [1], a high-resolution (~4 km) observationally constrained dataset, and derives standardized drought indices, precipitation statistics, temperature percentiles, and vapor pressure deficit (VPD) anomalies.

A key feature is the support for flexible climatological reference periods. Because a warming climate produces non-stationary baseline statistics, drought classifications derived from a fixed historical normal can misrepresent contemporary conditions [2] [3]. The system therefore supports both a rolling 30-year trailing window that tracks the contemporary climate and a full-period baseline that maximizes sample size.

All metrics are computed at each grid cell independently, across multiple aggregation timescales ranging from 15 to 730 days, as well as water-year and year-to-date accumulations.

2. Data Source

All meteorological inputs are obtained from gridMET [1], a daily surface meteorological dataset covering CONUS at approximately 4 km (1/24°) resolution. gridMET is produced by statistically downscaling NCEP/NCAR reanalysis fields using spatial climatological patterns from PRISM (Parameter-elevation Regressions on Independent Slopes Model), yielding a dataset that is both temporally consistent and spatially detailed. The archive extends from 1979 to near-present and is updated daily. Data are freely available at climatologylab.org/gridmet.html.

The following gridMET variables are used:

Precipitationprmm/daySPI, SPEI, precipitation metrics

Maximum air temperaturetmmxK (converted to °C)Temperature percentile

3. Drought Metrics

The four standardized drought indices each transform a climate variable into a standardized anomaly (z-score) by fitting a probability distribution to the historical climatological record and then applying a normal quantile transform. For a given aggregation window of W days, a rolling sum or mean of the input variable is computed over the full historical record. The resulting time series is stratified by calendar day-of-year, and a probability distribution is fitted to the subset of values within the selected reference period. The current observation is then transformed to a standardized anomaly using that fitted distribution.

Minimum data requirement: At least 10 years of data must fall within the selected reference period for a distribution fit to be attempted. Grid cells with fewer years receive a fill value of NA.

3.1 Standardized Precipitation Index (SPI)

The SPI [7] expresses precipitation anomalies in units of standard deviations relative to a fitted climatological distribution, making it comparable across locations and timescales with differing precipitation regimes. It is the most widely adopted standardized drought index.

Input & Aggregation

Daily precipitation (pr, mm). Rolling window sums over: 15, 30, 45, 60, 90, 120, 180, 365, and 730 days, plus water-year and year-to-date accumulations.

Statistical Method

Precipitation is bounded at zero and right-skewed. A two-parameter Gamma distribution (shape α, rate β) is fitted to the climatological sample using L-moment / probability-weighted moment (PWM) estimation [6]:

  1. Exact zeros are replaced with 0.01 mm to enable Gamma fitting.
  2. Unbiased probability-weighted moments are computed from the climatological sample.
  3. PWMs are converted to L-moments, from which Gamma parameters (α, β) are estimated.
  4. The Gamma CDF is evaluated at the current observation to obtain a non-exceedance probability p.
  5. SPI = Φ−1(p), the standard normal quantile transform.
SPI = Φ−1[ FΓ(α,β)( XW ) ]

3.2 Standardized Precipitation-Evapotranspiration Index (SPEI)

The SPEI [5] extends SPI by incorporating atmospheric evaporative demand via reference evapotranspiration (ETo). This makes it sensitive to warming-driven increases in PET even when precipitation is unchanged, capturing hydrological drought stress that SPI alone would miss.

Input & Aggregation

Water balance = precipitation (pr) − reference evapotranspiration (pet), in mm/day. Negative values indicate a moisture deficit. Rolling window means over the same timescales as SPI.

Statistical Method

The water balance can be negative and has heavier tails than precipitation alone. A three-parameter Generalized Logistic (GLO) distribution is used:

  1. L-moments are estimated from the climatological sample via unbiased PWMs.
  2. GLO parameters (ξ, α, k) are fitted from the L-moments.
  3. The GLO CDF is evaluated at the current observation → probability p.
  4. SPEI = Φ−1(p).
SPEI = Φ−1[ FGLO(ξ,α,k)( P − PET )W ]

Because it incorporates evaporative demand, SPEI typically shows stronger drying trends than SPI alone in a warming climate, consistent with the non-stationarity analyses of Diffenbaugh et al. [2] and Hoylman et al. [3].

3.3 Evaporative Demand Drought Index (EDDI)

EDDI [4] isolates the atmospheric demand component of the hydrological cycle. It is derived solely from reference evapotranspiration — without precipitation — and captures early warning signals of drought stress driven by high temperature, low humidity, or high wind speed before soil moisture depletion has occurred. EDDI is particularly valuable for flash drought early warning.

Input & Aggregation

Reference evapotranspiration (pet, mm/day). Rolling window sums (cumulative evaporative demand) over the same timescales as SPI.

Statistical Method

EDDI uses a nonparametric rank-based approach, avoiding assumptions about the distributional form of PET:

  1. Rank — Observations within the climatological sample are ranked from highest (rank 1) to lowest. Rank 1 corresponds to maximum evaporative demand (drought stress).
  2. Gringorten plotting position — The non-exceedance probability is estimated as:
    pi = (ranki − 0.33) / (n + 0.33)
    where n is the number of years in the reference period.
  3. Normal quantile transform — EDDI = Φ−1(p).
EDDI = Φ−1[ pGringorten( PETW ) ]

Sign convention: High PET (low p) produces positive EDDI values, indicating drought. This is the reverse of the SPI/SPEI convention where negative = dry; the reversal is handled in the dashboard color scale.

3.4 Standardized Vapor Pressure Deficit Index (SVPDI)

Vapor pressure deficit (VPD) is the difference between saturation and actual vapor pressure. Elevated VPD increases transpiration demand and can cause stomatal closure even when soil moisture is adequate, directly linking atmospheric drought to vegetation stress. The SVPDI provides a standardized measure of VPD anomaly complementary to precipitation-based indices. Its relationship to soil moisture and vegetation drought is evaluated in Hoylman et al. [3].

Input & Aggregation

Mean daily vapor pressure deficit (vpd, hPa). Rolling window means over the same timescales as SPI.

Statistical Method

VPD is non-negative and right-skewed, motivating the use of the Gamma distribution with L-moment/PWM parameter estimation, identical to the SPI procedure:

  1. Exact zeros are replaced with 0.001 hPa to enable Gamma fitting.
  2. Unbiased PWMs → L-moments → Gamma parameters → Gamma CDF → Φ−1(p).
SVPDI = Φ−1[ FΓ(α,β)( VPDW ) ]

Sign convention: Like EDDI, positive SVPDI = above-normal VPD = heightened atmospheric demand and potential vegetation stress. The dashboard color scale is reversed accordingly.

4. Precipitation Metrics

In addition to SPI, four derived precipitation metrics provide operational context using the same rolling aggregation windows and reference period:

Metric Formula Units Description
Percent of Normal ( XW / μclim ) × 100 % Current accumulation as a percentage of the climatological mean. Values below 100% indicate below-normal precipitation.
Departure XW − μclim inches Absolute anomaly relative to the climatological mean. Computed in mm from gridMET and converted to inches for display.
Percentile F̂n( XW ) 0–1 Empirical CDF rank within the climatological sample. Low values indicate unusually dry conditions.
Accumulation XW inches Raw rolling precipitation sum, converted from mm to inches. No comparison to a historical baseline.

The climatological mean (μclim) and empirical CDF are computed from the same reference period used for SPI, ensuring consistency across all precipitation products.

5. Temperature Metrics

Maximum Temperature Percentile

Anomalously high temperatures amplify drought through increased evapotranspiration and accelerated snowmelt. A percentile-based temperature metric provides context for heat as a drought driver without coupling it to a precipitation signal.

Input & Aggregation

Daily maximum air temperature (tmmx, converted from Kelvin to °C). Rolling window means over the standard timescales.

Statistical Method

A nonparametric empirical CDF is used — no parametric distribution is assumed:

  1. The empirical cumulative distribution function (ECDF) is constructed from the climatological sample for each grid cell and day-of-year.
  2. The ECDF is evaluated at the current observation to produce a probability on a 0–1 scale, where values near 1 indicate the warmest conditions on record.
Tmaxpctile = F̂n( TmaxW )

Values > 0.9 indicate exceptionally warm conditions (top decile of the reference climatology). The output is a probability rather than a z-score, making it directly interpretable as an exceedance percentile.

6. VPD Metrics

In addition to SVPDI, three derived VPD metrics parallel the precipitation metrics, providing multiple views of atmospheric dryness:

Metric Formula Units Description
VPD — Percent of Normal ( VPDW / μclim ) × 100 % Current VPD as a percentage of the climatological mean. Values above 100% indicate drier-than-normal atmospheric conditions.
VPD — Departure VPDW − μclim hPa Absolute anomaly relative to the climatological mean. Positive values indicate anomalously dry atmospheric conditions.
VPD — Percentile F̂n( VPDW ) 0–1 Empirical CDF rank within the climatological sample. High values indicate anomalously high atmospheric dryness.

VPD metrics share the same aggregation windows and reference period as the drought indices, providing a consistent framework for assessing atmospheric demand alongside precipitation supply.

7. Climatological Reference Periods

Standardized drought indices measure anomalies relative to a baseline climatology. The choice of baseline profoundly affects index values: the same precipitation total can indicate moderate drought against a 1950–1980 baseline but near-normal conditions against a 1991–2020 WMO normal. As the climate warms, the statistical properties of temperature, PET, and water balance trend systematically, violating the stationarity assumption embedded in fixed-period normals.

Diffenbaugh et al. [2] demonstrate that non-stationarity in baseline climate substantially alters drought frequency and severity assessments. Hoylman et al. [3] further argue that conventional drought assessment frameworks have been outpaced by the rate of climate change, motivating the use of contemporary reference periods for operational monitoring.

The dashboard provides two reference period options:

Mode Description
Rolling 30-year A trailing 30-year window ending at the current year. Tracks the contemporary climate, emphasizing recent trends. This is the default and recommended setting for operational drought monitoring.
Full period All years from 1979 to present. Maximizes sample size and provides a long-term historical perspective, but may overestimate drought severity in a warming climate due to non-stationarity.
Minimum data requirement: At least 10 years must fall within the selected reference period for any distribution fit to proceed. Grid cells that do not meet this threshold are assigned NA.
